So whats new in AI? I decided to take an AI refresher course thirty years later and this is what I found

I graduated with a degree in AI when the cost of the equivalent computational power to an iPhone was $50 million. A lot has changed but surprisingly much is still the same.

Stop the madness!

When I woke up this morning and checked my daily news feed of AI stories on Flipboard, I felt I should go back to sleep. It was clear that the world as we know it was about to end. In about a third of my personalised news articles the AI headlines screamed loud and clear. Heres a few of them:

AI experts list the real dangers of artificial intelligence
Limiting the downsides of artificial intelligence
New report outlines some of the more dystopian elements of artificial intelligence
New Report on Emerging AI Risks Paints a Grim Future
Elon Musk quits AI ethics research group
Will AI enslave the human race? Probably not, but it might jack you at the ATM
NatWest hires digital human Cora to chat to customers and help solve simple banking
Is artificial intelligence a threat to civilisation?

And when such brilliant minds as Stephen Hawking and Elon Musk say, and I quote, the development of full artificial intelligence could spell the end of the human race and artificial Intelligence is our biggest existential threat then who I am to argue. And when the prestigious McKinsey management consultancy that has the ear of the worlds leading CEOs, states commandingly we estimate that about half of all the activities people are paid to do in the worlds workforce could potentially be automated then it doesnt seem unreasonable to assume we are all doomed.

But in short, I believe this is a lot of media headline grabbing bullshit. And why am I qualified to opine on this topic? Well I have been watching AI for more than thirty years ever since I graduated from the University of Sussex with a degree in artificial intelligence and computer science . This was back in the days when people thought AI meant artificial insemination. And anyone who has lived, breathed and worked through the many chapters of AI history has a healthy degree of scepticism about the latest claims.

AI is a history of very cold winters

Artificial intelligence has blown hot and cold for as long as it has been around. In the 70s, 80s, 90s and even noughties massive hype and investment swirled around AI swiftly followed by a relegation to the hinterland as the expectations were invariably missed by a country mile. Trying to find a job with a degree in AI in the early 1990s was a sure way to unemployment. I had to reinvent myself as a geographical information system (GIS) engineer. At least GIS used AI algorithms such as the shortest route between two locations and knowledge representation techniques. But a lot of my fellow graduates in AI were not able to make a career from their educational choice unless they went into academia or research.

But something is really different now. AI might actually be starting to deliver on its promises. Not so quietly AI has crept into our daily lives with many daily services enabled by the technology:

  • Amazon Alexa, that $49 hockey puck, that sits in our kitchen can understand what I am saying and more often than not give me reasonable answers to my questions. Its speech to text recognition is really good. You should check out the associated app that has caught out many an unsuspecting teenager who have asked Alexa dubious questions not knowing that it is all converted into text for their parents to see.
  • Facebook is really good at facial recognition. Every day millions of photos are uploaded by its billions of users. Facebook scans those photos to find faces and then puts a name to them. It is not a feature that is seen as much in Europe due to privacy laws, but the technology is doing something at scale that has typically been the preserve of humans.
  • And Google demonstrated recently with its DeepMind AlphaGo team that a computer could beat the worlds best at the ancient game of Go. This was seen as impossible a few years back as the number of moves in the game outstrips the number of atoms in the universe. But their deep neural networks have been able to self-learn tactics that beat the worlds best.

And AI is powering all sorts of services from chatbots and virtual agents to smart robotics and autonomous vehicles. All of these are examples of AI demonstrating that computers can do activities that normally require human intelligence, such as visual perception or speech recognition, decision making, and sensing and navigating in a complex world.

We are living in an exponential world

When I studied AI the curriculum included speech recognition, natural language processing, visual recognition, machine learning, neural networks and reasoning. I remember an exam paper set us the challenge of how to detect car number license plates. And I wrote my first neural network to solve the travelling salesmen problemhow to find the shortest driving distance between ten geographic places. I remember a term long project programming a machine to learn how to do maths reasoning. But back then it really felt like we were a long way from anything that could be used in daily life. The programs were really slow. The data sets were really small. The systems couldnt generalise from one topic to another. And the so called artificial intelligence emphasised artificial over intelligence.

But something has changed. It is hard to imagine that the iPhone is only a little over ten years old. It is hard to imagine a world without Facebook. And it is hard to imagine a world where I just dont search Google if I need to find some information. The digital world has been enabled by an exponential increase in computing power and data. And this has driven the shift in AI. A few fun facts that testify to the power of our exponential world:

  • A car would be driving at 420,000,000 mph if its speed had improved at the same rate as computational chips since 1971.
  • Data storage is 28,450 cheaper today than it was in 1993.
  • It is estimated that by 2020 every single one of the seven billion persons on this planet will generate 1,700,000 bytes of data every second. That is the equivalent of 100s+ emails, without images, generated by every person on the planet every second of the day.

It is said that 80% of the worlds data has been generated just in the past two years. And it continues to increase exponentially driven by the growth in the internet of things, mobile devices, industrial sensors, fitness devices and social media activity. I had to look up the size of a Zettabyte (10 to the power of 21) as it is estimated that by 2020 there will be 44 Zettabytes of data generated.

This exponential world has given rise to AI applications that seem to work. Just over the past three years image recognition error rates have fallen from 28 to 7% with facial recognition much better. And speech recognition rates have dropped into human territory of accuracy.

Google gives it cutting edge machine learning software, TensorFlow, away for free

But there is also something else going on. It used to be really hard to write software to do things like machine learning. Machine learning is when the computer will learn for itself and not have to be told rules explicitly as you do when you write software.

Consider deep neural networks which have been the underlying technology driving much of the latest round of AI hysteria. Loosely modelled on the neurons in the brain, the schematic below shows a deep neural network that learns to recognise pictures of dogs and differentiates from similar animals such as wolves or cats.

The neural network is fed many pictures of animals during what is called supervised training. The neural network is shown a picture of an object and told whether it is a dog or not. The deep neural network consists of layers of neurons that represent increasing abstractions about the objects. The first layer finds basic visuals features such as line edges or textures or colours. The higher layers then start to put these together to see more holistic features such as a nose or a paw or a leg or a tail. Then the highest layers put them further together to see a horse or a dog or a wolf. In all these cases the machine has learnt the patterns itself after seeing many training examples. It can recognise with 90% accuracy whether a picture is a dog or a wolf.

The remarkable thing about this neural network software is that the machine has not been told what are the differences between animals but rather learnt itself. Rise the machines!

Writing software to do this was really hard. Getting machines to recognise objects, words or sentences would take me many months of graft and even then the results were limited. Thirty years ago we didnt have enough computer power to do multiple layers of learning, rather just a single layer, and we had tiny data sets.

Today Google is the worlds largest AI company. It is able to attract the best and brightest minds in the subject. They are working on autonomous vehicles, speech recognition, intelligent reasoning, massive search and most classic AI problems. And they have given their AI software powering their services away for free. Yes for free! TensorFlow is now open source software project that takes the best AI software libraries from Google and allows the community to use it pretty much encumbered.

You can setup a machine learning program in minutesyou are not a genius!

Not only does this TensorFlow software represent the state of the art in AI and machine learning, it is relatively straight forward to use. As a recent article in KDnuggets was headlined Neural network AI is simple. SoStop pretending you are a genius. The sentiment is somewhat fair. But the reality is that doing AI for prime time does take work and expertise. However getting a neural network demonstration up and running is pretty much childs play.

My favourite software library is Keras, which is again available for free. It wraps around Tensorflow and other machine learning engines making it even simpler to construct and train a neural network.

One of the first programs that AI students learn is MNIST. This is a task to get a machine to visually learn the difference between hand written digits zero to nine (below). A computer is shown 60,000 examples of images and learns to recognise the visual difference of these digits.

With Keras the code below is all you need to construct and train a deep neural networkthis is a convolutional neural network which is particularly well suited to vision recognition. The machines learns to recognise the digits with 99.6% accuracy.

The programming steps in Keras are as follows in the grey boxes. It is not important to understand the specific code instructions but rather note how that it requires only five major conceptual steps to implement the program.

  1. Load the software libraries for Keras along with matrices and vector maths
import numpy
from keras.datasets import mnist
from keras.models import Sequential
from keras.layers import Dense
from keras.layers import Dropout
from keras.layers import Flatten
from keras.layers.convolutional import Conv2D
from keras.layers.convolutional import MaxPooling2D
from keras.utils import np_utils
from keras import backend as K
K.set_image_dim_ordering('th')

2. Setup random numbers

# fix random seed for reproducibility
seed = 7
numpy.random.seed(seed)

3. Load the training data of 60,000 images and store them in correctly formed matrices and vectors

# load data
(X_train, y_train), (X_test, y_test) = mnist.load_data()
# reshape to be [samples][pixels][width][height]
X_train = X_train.reshape(X_train.shape[0], 1, 28, 28).astype('float32')
X_test = X_test.reshape(X_test.shape[0], 1, 28, 28).astype('float32')

4. Structure the input and output data to allow it to be used for training

# normalize inputs from 0-255 to 0-1
X_train = X_train / 255
X_test = X_test / 255
# one hot encode outputs
y_train = np_utils.to_categorical(y_train)
y_test = np_utils.to_categorical(y_test)
num_classes = y_test.shape[1]

5. Build the model, train it on the data and output the accuracy of the data

def baseline_model():
# create model
model = Sequential()
model.add(Conv2D(32, (5, 5), input_shape=(1, 28, 28), activation='relu'))
model.add(MaxPooling2D(pool_size=(2, 2)))
model.add(Dropout(0.2))
model.add(Flatten())
model.add(Dense(128, activation='relu'))
model.add(Dense(num_classes, activation='softmax'))
# Compile model
model.compile(loss='categorical_crossentropy', optimizer='adam', metrics=['accuracy'])
return model
# build the model
model = baseline_model()
# Fit the model
model.fit(X_train, y_train, validation_data=(X_test, y_test), epochs=10, batch_size=200, verbose=2)
# Final evaluation of the model
scores = model.evaluate(X_test, y_test, verbose=0)
print("CNN Error: %.2f%%" % (100-scores[1]*100))

AI will be a ubiquitous enabling toolkit

Ihave been rolling up my sleeves and getting my hands really dirty over the past few months programming neural networks. One afternoon I had a sudden sense of deja vu followed by a light bulb moment.

About thirty years ago the first commercial SQL relational databases appeared from the likes of IBM, Ingres and Oracle. Databases would store, manage and allow analysis of data structured in a tabular format. They didnt provide any clear end user benefits in their own right, but they were a set of technologies and tools that enabled businesses to create endless applications. Today I am hard pressed to think of a single company globally that does not have applications that are enabled by relational, and now non-SQL, databases. It has become ubiquitous and the number of applications they enable is simply a function of the limit of imagination.

In the same way the development of the AI market today is being driven by incredibly powerful and accessible AI toolkits and technologies. These toolkits allow endless applications to be build. DeepIndex allows you to view nearly 300 applications of AI most of which would have been built with these toolkits.

The ease with which these tools can be used to enable valuable applications will mean that we might not even talk about AI and machine learning in the coming years as it will be so common and woven into the fabric of all technologies.

And AI will prove real economic value across thousands of use cases

Iwanted to know about the economic value of AI now. So I spent the past few months mapping hundreds of use cases for AI and looking at hundreds of AI companies along with tons of technology toolkits and frameworks. I wanted to know how AI is now being used across company departments and across companies and industries. And I wanted to know whether AI has real value. I was hard pushed to identify a single function and industry that would not be using AI in the coming years.

While much of the corporate use of AI is in the planning and experimental phase today, the business use of AI will start with helping to better manage and analyse the wealth of corporate data. Machine learning is fantastic at spotting patterns in massive data sets. Its application will allow companies to increase revenues, reduce costs, enhance productivity and improve product and service offerings. Some of the tasks that machine learning is really good at include:

  • Recognizing visual patterns such as identifying faces and objects in scenes, detecting visually defects on the manufacturing line, spotting cancer markers on pathology slides, or estimating the damage to a car from inspection photos or determining when a brand logo is being used without a license.
  • Predictions such as which customers are likely to churn, which consumers are most likely to convert to paid customers or determining when parts should be replaced to provide predictive maintenance. Or predicting which credit card transactions are likely to be fraud. Or which movies will a person like based on recommendations from others. And which activity in our network is a cybersecurity breach.
  • Speech and natural language processing such as automatically screening and ranking resumes / CVs or determining the sentiment of a customer to help call center agents better respond to customers.

And of course there is a whole set of use cases around sensing and reacting technologies such as robotics and autonomous vehicles.

So who is going to make money from AI?

In examining AI uses cases it became clear that we are now entering a truely golden era for AI. Thousands of new companies are racing to seize their share of the pie. I will save my analysis for another paper but there are a few key themes appearing as to where the economic value is now migrating:

  • Data is the key to AI. To train machines to be smart it is critical to have data training sets. And those with the data sets are in an advantageous position. Whereas a few years ago an upstart such as Uber or AirBnB could upend whole industries, now some of the value and power shifts back to the incumbent corporates. Who is better placed to take advantage of AI than the insurance company that has reams of historic data on underwriting claims? The financial services company that knows everything about consumer buying behaviour? Or the search company that sees more user searches for information than any other?
  • The global technology giants are in arms race for leadership in AI. According to CBInsights over $20B was invested by the tech giants in AI in 2016. Google is arguably the worlds largest AI company. But it is in a battle with Amazon, Microsoft and IBM for predominance. There are few opportunities for AI start-ups to upend the AI infrastructure and platforms that are the focus of these giants. Few startups are going to outspend or outsmart Microsoft on cloud computing or build a better AI chip than Googles Tensor Processing Unit or build better object recognition software libraries than Amazon.
  • Countries are also in an AI arms race. China has not been shy about its call to arms around AI. It believes that it has structural advantages over the US including a more supportive regulatory framework (especially in data privacy and use), its government backed AI eco-system includes a huge investment in growing technical and engineering talent, along with the massive size and availability of data. Chinese AI companies such as Baidu or Face++ are really good and have massive data sets. The Chinese point out that their mobile market is 3x the size of the US and there are 50x more mobile payments taking placethis is a massive data advantage.
  • AI start-ups abound but they need to have access to unique data sets, deep domain knowledge, deep pockets and an ability to attract the increasingly in-demand AI talent. This is not a case of an app in a garage will change the world. Over $6B was invested in AI companies by VC and PE in 2016, most of which was in machine learning horizontal applications.

The gold-rush is on for AI. But increasingly it looks like the biggest companies and biggest countries will capture the lions share of the economic value.

Final reflectionslearning about AI is both easier and harder

I decided to take a research sabbatical over the past few months to learn about AI. I have really wanted to make sense of what is for real and what is not. Learning about the current state of AI is very much a big data problem. The volume, variety and velocity of information about what is going on is huge. And this reflects an industry that is truly booming. Keeping up with the latest AI research, technologies, tools, start-up companies, use cases is like drinking from a fire hose. If you do a Google search for neural networks you are presented with 1.4M articles. With an acronym soup of deep neural networks, capsule networks, Convolutional networks, LSTM, , BatchNorm, activation functions, stochastic gradient descent, dimensionality reduction, embedding, dropouts, residual networks, Keras, Tensorflow, recurrent neural networks (RNNs), residual networks, data wrangling, Python, kernels, feed forward, supervised Vs unsupervised and so on, it is easy to become overwhelmed. The internet has proven a true boom to being able to find information but the problem is that there is too much information.

So I decided to go back to school. I needed to go back to a classroom of a trusted and respected institution that is known for its pedagogics. So what did I do? I signed up to a massive open online learning course (MOOCs). I chose the Coursera Deep Learning course taught by Andrew Ng who is an adjunct professor at Stanford University and had worked at Google and Baidu. He is a brilliant instructor who understands the pedagogics of teaching AI. And the cost was simply my time and monthly fees less than the cost of your Starbucks coffee. MOOCs have democratised education and this is a massive change from my first AI course some thirty years ago.

The online courses were fun and challenging. I found that they reactivated parts of my brain where my knowledge of derivatives and calculus, massively dimensional matrix and vector spaces, programming (now Python) and machine learning algorithms unexpectedly lay dormant. It came back. And the material was frighteningly familiar some several decades later. And the programming was certainly a lot easier than it used to bethere are hundreds of libraries that take care of all the difficult bits.

The more things change, the more they stay the same

AI will have a real impact on the work place but we should ignore the hyperbole. There will ultimately be displacement in jobs but for the most part AI will prove to be a boom for productivity. Yes, there are genuine worries with AI such as the digital risks of hacking, physical risks such as remote attacks by autonomous drones, and political risks from fake propaganda and state surveillance. But the machines are not anywhere close to rising to the level of general intelligence where they truly supersede our levels. When a neural network today learns about the visual difference between a cat and a dog it doesnt have the human like ability to generalise and hold any concept of these animals. It doesnt know that they are alive. That they are pets. That they should be stroked gently. That they should be fed. That they have feelings. It knows little.

Thirty years ago we studied the concept of general intelligence and narrow intelligence. General intelligence is still a long way off but narrow has improved by leaps and bonds. What has really changed in AI is that that the tools, technologies and algorithms have really evolved. It is now a hundred or more times easier to implement AI at scale. And a lot lot cheaper. And the economic opportunities of AI are now large and exciting. There is a gold-rush going on and once again the economic benefits will be captured by the few and the large.

But if you ask me what has changed in thirty years. Everything has, but conceptually not much has really changed at all.
